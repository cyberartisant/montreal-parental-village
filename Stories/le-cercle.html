Le Cercle Saint-Joseph

How five single parents, a borrowed kitchen table, and a pot of too-strong coffee quietly rewrote the map of family support in Montréal.

calendar_today Le Plateau-Mont-Royal, Montréal schedule 8 min read arrow_back Back to Home

Chapter One

The night it rained and nobody left

It started, as so many things on the Plateau do, with bad weather and a refusal to go home.

It was a Tuesday in late November — the kind of evening where the sleet comes in sideways and the sidewalks on rue Saint-Joseph Est gleam like old mirrors. Nadia Tremblay-Côté had just put her daughter to bed and opened the door to let her neighbour in before the wind could. That neighbour, Oumar Diallo, was holding a sleeping three-year-old against his shoulder and a bottle of wine he no longer had a free hand to open.

They had met at the dépanneur six months earlier, the way all true friendships on the Plateau begin — arguing politely over the last good avocado. Oumar had let her have it. Nadia had never quite forgiven herself for taking it.

By ten o'clock the rain hadn't stopped and two more neighbours had knocked. Saoirse Gallagher-Morin from the third floor, who was a nurse and worked nights and had perfected the art of sleeping in four-hour intervals. And then old Jules Lafortune — not old, really, forty-six, but the kind of person who seemed to have always existed — who had moved back to the Plateau after a divorce and was raising twin boys in a five-and-a-half that smelled of turpentine and pasta.

They sat around Nadia's kitchen table. The children were asleep. Someone opened the wine. And for the first time in longer than any of them could exactly remember, nobody performed being fine.

"I kept waiting for someone to ask if I needed help. After a while I realized everyone was waiting for the same thing."

— Nadia Tremblay-Côté, co-founder

They talked until two in the morning. About the specific exhaustion that has no name — the one that isn't tiredness exactly, but the weight of being the only adult in a home. About the impossibility of being sick. About the guilt of wanting, sometimes, just one evening where no one needed anything from you.

Nobody solved anything that night. But before they left, Oumar said: "Same time next week?" And everyone said yes.

Chapter Two

The founding five

Within a month there were five regulars. The group had no name, no structure, and no agenda, which everyone agreed was exactly right.

NT

Nadia Tremblay-Côté

Graphic designer, mother of Léa, 6. Grew up in Rimouski, moved to Plateau for love, stayed for the bagels.

OD

Oumar Diallo

Software developer, father of Amadou, 3. Came from Dakar via Bordeaux. Makes the best thiéboudienne in two arrondissements.

SG

Saoirse Gallagher-Morin

Night-shift nurse at Hôtel-Dieu, mother of Finn, 8. Irish-Québécoise. Has strong opinions about sleep and weather.

JL

Jules Lafortune

Scenery painter, father of Baptiste and Simon, 9. Cooks in large quantities. Knows every hardware store within five métro stops.

PB

Priya Balasubramanian

Urban planner for the Ville, mother of Arun, 4. Joined after Oumar left a handwritten note under her door. Became its quiet architect.

It was Priya — methodical, warm, and constitutionally incapable of letting a good idea stay disorganized — who first suggested they might actually be onto something. She had read studies about isolation among single-parent households. She had seen the numbers in her own planning work: the Plateau had one of the city's highest concentrations of monoparental families, and almost no infrastructure built specifically for them.

"We're not a support group," Jules protested, refilling his glass. "Support groups have facilitators and worksheets."

"We could just be a circle," said Nadia. "Le Cercle."

It stuck.

Chapter Three

The thing nobody expected: it worked

By spring, word had spread the way it only can on the Plateau — through conversations at Marché Jean-Talon, at the parc Laurier sandbox, in the queue at the caisse populaire. Someone told someone. That someone texted three others.

The group outgrew Nadia's kitchen. They migrated to the salle communautaire of a church on rue Gilford that rented its basement for twenty dollars a night and asked no questions about wine. There were twelve parents. Then nineteen. Then, by June, thirty-one.

They were careful about what they were. It was not therapy. It was not a co-op. It was not a formal network. It was, as Priya described it in the Google Doc she used to keep notes that no one had asked her to keep: "a recurring dinner party where the real agenda is survival."

But certain things happened organically that began to look, from the outside, like structure. A shared spreadsheet emerged — started by Oumar at midnight during a particularly bad week — that listed who could do what and when. Emergency childcare swaps. A ride to a medical appointment. A meal left at a door when someone had the flu. Someone who could fix a leaking faucet. Someone who had a car and didn't mind driving to IKEA.

"We called it the spreadsheet. It had no name. It became, I think, the most useful document in my life after my child's health card."

— Oumar Diallo, co-founder

Chapter Four

The summer of difficult questions

Growth brought friction. This, too, was expected — or would have been, if anyone had expected any of this.

The question of money arrived that summer, as it always does. Priya had quietly applied for a small grant from the arrondissement — two thousand dollars, enough to cover the church basement for a year and print some flyers. She told the group at the July meeting. Jules was uneasy. "The moment we take money," he said, "we have to answer to something other than each other."

They debated it for three weeks, across two meetings and a very long group text thread. In the end, they took the grant. And Jules was right and wrong in equal measure: it did change things. But the change was, mostly, good. Priya used forty dollars of it to buy a proper coffee urn, and that alone was worth it.

The harder question came in August, when a parent arrived at a meeting in crisis — not the comfortable, manageable kind of tired they all shared, but something deeper and more urgent. Saoirse, who had seen this before, sat with her until midnight. Afterward, the group talked about what they were and weren't equipped to handle.

They reached out to a social worker named Dominique who lived two streets over and had been coming to meetings for two months without ever quite joining. She said she'd been hoping they'd ask.

Chapter Five

How a Plateau kitchen table became a city

The call came in October of the second year, from a parent in Rosemont who had read a small piece about Le Cercle in a neighbourhood newsletter. Then from Verdun. Then from Saint-Henri, where a school social worker had heard about the spreadsheet and wanted to know if it could work in her community.

Priya drove to every meeting. She brought the same Google Doc, the same principles they had landed on after that difficult summer, and the same advice: don't start with a structure, start with a table and too much coffee.

November — Year 1

Rue Saint-Joseph Est — five parents, one kitchen table, one bottle of wine

Spring — Year 1

Église Gilford basement — 31 members, one shared spreadsheet, the first emergency childcare swap

Summer — Year 1

First grant — arrondissement funding, coffee urn, a social worker named Dominique

Year 2

Rosemont, Verdun, Saint-Henri — three new circles, same model, different kitchens

Year 3

Le Réseau des Cercles — eleven arrondissements, 400 families, one shared online platform built by Oumar on a weekend

Year 4

City of Montréal partnership — official recognition, a real budget, a coordinator, and a small office on Avenue du Parc that always smells of coffee

They were written up, eventually, in Le Devoir. A researcher from UQAM studied them for two years and wrote a paper whose title — L'infrastructure informelle du soin : réseaux monoparentaux en milieu urbain — made Saoirse laugh until she cried. Jules had it framed and hung it in his studio, next to a drawing Baptiste made of "dad's coffee friends."

When asked, in interview after interview, what the secret was, each of the five founders gave a different answer. Priya talked about trust built slowly, person by person. Oumar talked about the spreadsheet — always the spreadsheet. Jules said there was no secret, only showing up. Saoirse said it was knowing what you couldn't do. And Nadia — who still hosted the original Plateau circle in her apartment when everyone could fit, which was rarer now — said it simply:

"We stopped waiting for someone else to build what we needed. We just made it ourselves, the only way you can — badly, at first, and together."

— Nadia Tremblay-Côté

Outside, on rue Saint-Joseph Est, the sleet had started again. It does that, in November. The lights were on in the windows of a hundred apartments, each one its own small world, a little less alone than it used to be.
